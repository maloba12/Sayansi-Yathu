TOPIC 1.1.0: CONCEPTS AND METHODS IN BIOLOGY
Experiment 1: Scientific Inquiry Method
Topic: Nature of Science Inquiry in Biology
Form: 1
Aim: To apply the scientific inquiry method in carrying out scientific investigations.
Materials: Notebook, pen, simple objects from the local environment (e.g., plant leaf, stone, insect).
Procedure:

Observe a biological phenomenon in the surroundings.
Identify a problem or question.
Form a hypothesis.
Test the hypothesis through simple experimentation or observation.
Record and analyse data.
Form a conclusion and share findings with peers.
Observation: Stages of inquiry followed; data recorded clearly.
Conclusion: Scientific inquiry method leads to reliable understanding of biological facts.
Safety Precautions: Handle living or sharp objects carefully; work in a clean area.

Experiment 2: Characteristics of Living Things
Topic: Characteristics of Living Things
Form: 1
Aim: To investigate the characteristics of living things.
Materials: Living specimens (e.g., plant, insect, earthworm) and non-living objects (e.g., stone).
Procedure:

Observe living and non-living specimens.
Test for each characteristic (feeding/nutrition, respiration, excretion, reproduction, sensitivity/irritability, movement/locomotion, growth).
Distinguish living things from non-living things.
Observation: Living things show all seven characteristics; non-living things do not.
Conclusion: Living organisms exhibit nutrition, respiration, excretion, reproduction, sensitivity, movement, and growth.
Safety Precautions: Do not harm living organisms; wash hands after handling specimens.

TOPIC 1.2.0: PRINCIPLES OF CELLULAR LIFE
Experiment 3: Use of Microscopes
Topic: Microscopes
Form: 1
Aim: To use different types of microscopes to examine specimens.
Materials: Compound microscope, prepared slides or simple specimens (e.g., onion epidermis), glass slides, cover slips, water, dropper.
Procedure:

Identify parts of the compound microscope and their functions.
Prepare a wet mount specimen slide.
Mount the slide, focus under low and high power.
Observe and calculate magnification.
Observation: Cells and structures become visible at different magnifications.
Conclusion: Microscopes allow detailed examination of small biological specimens.
Safety Precautions: Handle microscope and glass slides carefully to avoid breakage or eye injury.

Experiment 4: Basic Cell Structure
Topic: Basic Cell Structure
Form: 1
Aim: To explore the basic cell structure.
Materials: Microscope, onion epidermis or cheek cells, slides, cover slips, methylene blue or iodine stain.
Procedure:

Prepare a wet mount of plant or animal cells.
Stain and observe under microscope.
Illustrate and label the basic structures.
Create a simple model of a cell.
Observation: Plant cells show cell wall and chloroplasts; animal cells show nucleus and cytoplasm.
Conclusion: Cells have basic structures that perform life functions.
Safety Precautions: Use stains carefully; avoid direct contact with eyes.

Experiment 5: Types of Cells (Eukaryotic vs Prokaryotic)
Topic: Types of Cells
Form: 1
Aim: To distinguish between eukaryotic and prokaryotic cells.
Materials: Microscope, prepared slides of plant/animal cells and bacterial cells (or simple bacterial smear).
Procedure:

Observe eukaryotic cells (plant, animal, fungi, amoeba).
Observe prokaryotic cells (bacterial).
Compare structures and functions of organelles.
Observation: Eukaryotic cells have nucleus and membrane-bound organelles; prokaryotic cells do not.
Conclusion: Eukaryotic and prokaryotic cells differ in structure and complexity.
Safety Precautions: Handle microscope slides gently.

Experiment 6: Specialised Cells
Topic: Cell Specialisation
Form: 1
Aim: To relate adaptive features of specialised cells to their functions.
Materials: Microscope, slides or pictures/models of specialised cells (root hair, palisade, guard, nerve, blood, muscle, sperm cells).
Procedure:

Observe adaptive features of each specialised cell.
Relate features to their specific functions.
Observation: Each cell has unique structures suited to its role (e.g., root hair cell has long extension for absorption).
Conclusion: Specialised cells have adaptive features that enable them to perform specific functions efficiently.
Safety Precautions: Handle slides and models with care.

TOPIC 1.3.0: MAINTENANCE OF THE ORGANISM
Experiment 7: Nutritional Deficiency Diseases and Disorders
Topic: Nutritional Deficiency Diseases and Disorders
Form: 1
Aim: To investigate nutritional deficiency diseases and disorders in plants and human beings.
Materials: Pictures or real examples of deficient plants (e.g., yellow leaves for chlorosis), charts of human deficiency symptoms.
Procedure:

Observe symptoms of deficiencies in plants and humans.
Identify missing nutrients (e.g., nitrogen, iron, vitamins).
Recommend corrective nutrients or treatments.
Observation: Specific symptoms linked to lack of particular nutrients.
Conclusion: Appropriate nutrients can address deficiency diseases in plants and humans.
Safety Precautions: Do not consume any experimental materials.

TOPIC 1.4.0: CONTINUITY OF LIFE
Experiment 8: Reproduction in Living Organisms
Topic: Reproduction in Living Organisms
Form: 1
Aim: To demonstrate understanding of how living organisms reproduce.
Materials: Seeds, spores (e.g., from ferns), vegetative parts (e.g., potato tuber, onion bulb), Rhizopus/Mucor on bread.
Procedure:

Demonstrate asexual reproduction using vegetative parts, spores, and seeds.
Demonstrate sexual reproduction using seeds.
Compare asexual and sexual reproduction.
Grow fungi (Rhizopus/Mucor) on food to observe structure.
Observation: New plants/organisms grow from vegetative parts or spores without gametes; fungi show spore formation.
Conclusion: Living organisms reproduce asexually or sexually; fungi reproduce by spores.
Safety Precautions: Do not eat mouldy food; wash hands after handling.

TOPIC 1.5.0: COORDINATION AND RESPONSE
Experiment 9: Tropisms in Plants
Topic: Coordination and Response in Plants
Form: 1
Aim: To explore tropisms in plants and their relevance.
Materials: Young seedlings (beans or maize), boxes with light from one side, water, chemicals (e.g., salt solution), pots.
Procedure:

Set up shoots and roots for phototropism (light from one side).
Set up for geotropism (gravity).
Set up for hydrotropism (water) and chemotropism (chemicals).
Observe and record direction of growth over days.
Observation: Shoots grow towards light; roots grow towards gravity/water/chemicals.
Conclusion: Tropisms help plants respond to environmental stimuli for survival.
Safety Precautions: Use safe, non-toxic chemicals; water plants regularly.

Experiment 10: Taxic Responses in Invertebrates
Topic: Coordination and Response in Animals
Form: 1
Aim: To explore taxic responses in invertebrates and their relevance.
Materials: Woodlice, cockroaches or earthworms, simple choice chamber or dark/light setup, chemicals.
Procedure:

Place invertebrates in setups with light vs dark, moist vs dry, or chemical gradients.
Record direction of movement (phototaxis, geotaxis, chemotaxis).
Evaluate relevance to survival.
Observation: Invertebrates move towards or away from stimuli (e.g., woodlice prefer dark/moist).
Conclusion: Taxic responses help invertebrates avoid danger or find favourable conditions.
Safety Precautions: Handle invertebrates gently and release them unharmed.

TOPIC 1.6.0: ECOLOGICAL RELATIONSHIPS AND EVOLUTION
Experiment 11: Features of Ecosystems
Topic: Ecosystem
Form: 1
Aim: To explore features of ecosystems in the local environment.
Materials: Notebook, measuring tape, quadrat (if available) or marked area in school ground/pond.
Procedure:

Visit a local terrestrial or aquatic ecosystem.
Observe and record biotic and abiotic features.
Construct a simple model of the ecosystem.
Observation: Presence of producers, consumers, decomposers, soil, water, light, etc.
Conclusion: Ecosystems consist of interacting biotic and abiotic components.
Safety Precautions: Stay in safe areas; do not disturb habitats unnecessarily.

Experiment 12: Soil Composition and Fertility
Topic: The Soil – Composition and Fertility
Form: 1
Aim: To analyse soil composition and fertility.
Materials: Soil samples (clay, sandy, loamy), water, beakers, filter paper, potted plants or seeds.
Procedure:

Examine soil components (particles, air, humus, microorganisms).
Test properties (capillarity, drainage, particle size).
Compare crop/seed growth in different soil types.
Investigate ways to improve fertility.
Observation: Loamy soil shows best drainage and plant growth; different soils have different textures and fertility.
Conclusion: Soil fertility depends on composition; it can be improved through good practices.
Safety Precautions: Wash hands after handling soil; use clean equipment.
